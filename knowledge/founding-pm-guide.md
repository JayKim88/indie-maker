# Founding PM Guide

Reference for the indie-maker planning agent.
Covers the methodologies, frameworks, and non-negotiable rules a Founding PM applies
when taking a product from idea to first paying customer.

Sources: Steve Blank (Customer Development), Eric Ries (Lean Startup),
Clayton Christensen (JTBD), Rob Fitzpatrick (The Mom Test),
Andrew Chen (Distribution), Patrick McKenzie (Pricing), Paul Graham (Essays).

---

## Non-Negotiable Rules

Rules a 20-year Founding PM enforces before writing a single line of code.

### Customer Development (Steve Blank)

1. **No facts exist inside the building** — All assumptions about the customer are hypotheses until confirmed by real people. An idea is not a product. A conversation is not a sale. A "great idea" from a friend is not market validation.

2. **Problem before solution** — Never pitch the solution in a customer interview. Ask only about the problem, past behavior, and context. The moment you pitch, you contaminate the data. People will tell you what you want to hear.

3. **Behavior over opinion** — "Would you use this?" is worthless. "Tell me about the last time you dealt with this problem" is gold. Past behavior predicts future behavior; stated preferences do not.

4. **5 interviews minimum before any build decision** — If you haven't spoken to 5 potential customers, you are building on assumption. No exceptions. Talking to family and friends who "support your idea" does not count.

5. **Earlyvangelists only** — The only customers worth finding first are those who: (a) have the problem, (b) know they have it, (c) are already trying to solve it, and (d) have budget. Everyone else is noise at the validation stage.

### The Mom Test (Rob Fitzpatrick)

6. **Never ask "would you use this?"** — People are polite. They say yes to avoid hurting your feelings. The Mom Test: only ask questions that even your mom can't lie to you about, because they're grounded in their own past behavior.

7. **Compliments are a bad sign** — "This is a great idea!" means nothing. "I tried to solve this last month and spent $200 on [X]" means everything. Filter out compliments, probe for specific past actions and spending.

8. **Push for commitment signals** — Real validation requires skin in the game: pre-payment, a letter of intent, an introduction to a decision-maker, or a scheduled follow-up demo. "I'd definitely use it" with no commitment attached = rejection.

### Riskiest Assumption Test (RAT)

9. **Name the single riskiest assumption first** — Every product idea rests on a tower of assumptions. Find the one that, if wrong, kills the entire business. That is the only assumption worth testing in week one. Do not build anything else until it is validated.

10. **Smoke test before build** — A landing page with a waitlist signup, a pre-order button, or a manual concierge version of the product tests demand without writing application code. If nobody signs up for a landing page, nobody will pay for the real product.

11. **Falsifiable hypothesis format** — Every assumption must be stated as: "We believe [user segment] will [do X action] because [reason]. We will know this is true when [measurable outcome] within [timeframe]." Vague assumptions cannot be tested or falsified.

### Jobs-to-be-Done (Clayton Christensen)

12. **Three job types, all matter** — Functional job (what they're trying to accomplish), Emotional job (how they want to feel), Social job (how they want to be perceived). Products that nail only the functional job get copied. Products that nail all three build loyalty.

13. **Hire/fire framing** — Users don't "adopt" products; they hire them to do a job and fire the previous solution. Ask: "What were you using before? Why did you fire it? What triggered the switch?" This reveals the real switching cost and true competitive set.

14. **Situation, not segment** — Target users by situation, not demographics. "Stressed professionals who have a 4pm deadline and need to make a decision fast" is more actionable than "35-44 year old knowledge workers." Situations drive purchase; demographics do not.

### Distribution Strategy (Andrew Chen, a16z)

15. **Distribution is harder than product** — The #1 cause of startup failure is not a bad product — it is a product nobody finds. Define the distribution channel before the product is built. If you cannot name where the first 100 users will come from, the product is not ready to build.

16. **One channel to PMF, then diversify** — Pick one acquisition channel and go deep until it works. Spreading across SEO + Twitter + Reddit + cold email simultaneously means none of them work. After PMF, diversify. Before PMF, focus.

17. **Channel-product fit matters as much as product-market fit** — The best channel for your product is determined by where your early adopters already congregate and what content/format they consume. A B2B enterprise tool distributed via viral consumer loops will not work.

18. **Embedded distribution > paid distribution** — Virality, referral mechanics, SEO content, and integrations compound over time with zero marginal cost. Paid ads require continuous spending. Build distribution that works while you sleep, before paying for growth.

### Pricing Strategy (Patrick McKenzie, OpenView Partners)

19. **Price is a product decision, not a sales decision** — Pricing communicates value. Low pricing signals low quality to many B2B buyers. Set price based on value delivered, not cost of goods. "How much does it cost to build?" is irrelevant to pricing.

20. **Willingness-to-Pay (WTP) must be measured, not guessed** — Ask: "What do you currently spend to solve this problem (time + money)?" and "At what price would this be so cheap you'd question the quality? So expensive you'd walk away?" The gap between these is your pricing range.

21. **Charge from day one** — Free products attract users who will never pay. Charging from day one, even a small amount, filters for real buyers and generates the revenue signal needed for Kill/Go decisions. Free plans should exist to drive conversion, not as a permanent tier.

22. **Raise prices before you think you should** — Indie makers systematically underprice because they fear rejection. If less than 20% of prospects push back on price, you are priced too low. Price pushback rate of 20-30% is healthy.

### PMF (Product-Market Fit) Signals

23. **Sean Ellis 40% test is the PMF benchmark** — Survey active users: "How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?" If ≥ 40% answer "very disappointed," you have PMF. Below 40% = pre-PMF, keep iterating. This single number predicts retention and word-of-mouth better than NPS.

24. **Retention curve shape tells the truth** — A flat retention curve (stabilizes above 0% after initial drop) = PMF potential. A curve that trends to zero = no PMF regardless of acquisition numbers. Never grow before achieving a flat retention curve.

25. **PMF feels like pull, not push** — Before PMF, every user is a struggle to acquire and every engagement is forced. After PMF, users pull the product forward: they complain when features are missing, they bring colleagues, they tweet without being asked. You feel the difference.

### Anti-Patterns (What Founding PMs Never Do)

26. **Never build in stealth** — Stealth prevents the feedback loop that validates whether you are building the right thing. Sharing the idea does not get it stolen; execution and distribution are the moat, not the concept.

27. **Never optimize before validating** — Premature optimization: perfecting onboarding before anyone signs up, building admin dashboards before any users, designing a mobile app before validating the web version. Every hour optimizing an unvalidated product is waste.

28. **Never mistake activity for progress** — Lines of code written, features shipped, design iterations completed — these are vanity metrics before PMF. The only metric that matters pre-PMF is: "Did a new person pay for this today and come back tomorrow?"

29. **Never let the backlog drive the roadmap** — A backlog filled by user requests without validation is a list of opinions. Every backlog item must be tied to a validated problem, a measurable impact hypothesis, and an AARRR stage. Features that don't move a metric don't belong in the sprint.

30. **Never skip the distribution conversation** — "We'll figure out marketing later" is how products die with great reviews and zero users. Distribution strategy is a Phase 0 question, not a Phase 5 question.

## Core Frameworks Quick Reference

### Customer Development Interview Script

```
Opening:
"Tell me about the last time you dealt with [problem area]."

Follow-up probes:
"Walk me through exactly what happened."
"How often does this happen?"
"What did you try to solve it?"
"How much did that cost you — in time and money?"
"What was the biggest frustration with that solution?"

Commitment test:
"We're building something for this problem. Would you be willing to
 [pay $X for beta access / join our pilot / connect us with 2 others]?"

What NOT to ask:
"Would you use this?" / "What features do you want?" / "Do you like this idea?"
```

### Distribution Channel Evaluation

```
For each channel candidate, score 1-5:

| Channel | Reach | CAC | Payback | Control | Score |
|---------|-------|-----|---------|---------|-------|
| SEO/Content | 5 | 1 | Slow | 4 | — |
| Reddit/Community | 3 | 1 | Fast | 2 | — |
| ProductHunt | 4 | 1 | One-shot | 3 | — |
| Cold email/outbound | 2 | 3 | Fast | 5 | — |
| Partnerships/Integrations | 4 | 2 | Medium | 3 | — |
| Paid ads | 5 | 5 | Medium | 5 | — |

Founding PM rule: Choose the channel where you have:
1. Unfair access (existing audience, domain credibility, relationships), OR
2. The highest ratio of Reach × Control ÷ CAC
```

### WTP (Willingness-to-Pay) Interview

```
Step 1 — Anchor on current spend:
"What do you currently spend (time + money) solving this problem?"

Step 2 — Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity:
"At what price is this too cheap to trust the quality?"
"At what price does it start to feel expensive but you'd still consider it?"
"At what price is it definitely too expensive?"

Step 3 — Commitment test:
"At $[price from step 2], would you pay right now?"
→ Yes with credit card out = validated
→ "Probably" = not validated
→ No = price too high OR problem not acute enough

WTP range: between "too cheap" and "starts to feel expensive"
Optimal price: 80th percentile of WTP range
```

### North Star Metric Framework

```
North Star = single metric that best captures value delivered to customers
             AND predicts long-term revenue

Bad North Stars:
  Signups    → don't predict retention
  DAU        → doesn't capture value
  Revenue    → lags behind product health

Good North Stars (examples):
  Slack:     "Messages sent per active team"
  Airbnb:    "Nights booked"
  Duolingo:  "Days of streak maintained"
  B2B SaaS:  "Features used per active user per week"

Formula:
  North Star = [Core Action] × [Frequency] that indicates [Value Delivered]

For indie makers, pick the metric that answers:
  "If this number goes up consistently, are users definitely getting value?"
```

## Quick Reference Checklist

Run before declaring "ready to build":

**Validation**
- [ ] Spoke to ≥ 5 potential customers (not friends/family)
- [ ] Collected specific past behavior stories — not opinions
- [ ] At least 1 person offered pre-payment or strong commitment signal
- [ ] Riskiest assumption named and experiment designed to test it
- [ ] Smoke test completed (landing page, pre-order, or concierge MVP)

**Market**
- [ ] Distribution channel identified with unfair access or clear path
- [ ] Competitor set defined via "hire/fire" lens (what are users firing to use this?)
- [ ] WTP range measured from real interview data — not guessed

**Product**
- [ ] JTBD defined: functional + emotional + social job
- [ ] North Star Metric defined before building
- [ ] Core scenario = exactly 1 (the one that proves the product works)
- [ ] "Won't build" list is longer than "will build" list

**Business**
- [ ] Charging from day one is the default — free tier has conversion rationale
- [ ] Kill criteria have explicit numbers tied to validation assumptions
- [ ] Solo buildable in ≤ 2 weeks confirmed (scope or tech adjusted if not)
